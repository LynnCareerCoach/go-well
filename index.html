THE STORY OF THE Four World Views MAP

The "flat earth conspiracy" was probably conceived by students in a mapmaking class, because representing the earth's features on a flat surface is about as easy as peeling an orange without tearing the rind.

Making a world map involves distorting the surface of the sphere that is Earth so it conforms to a two-dimensional rectangle. You may preserve accuracy in direction (useful for ships navigating the ocean), but then you must sacrifice size proportionality (say, the size of South America compared to Greenland). When you choose a Coordinate Reference System, you're choosing what your priority is and what you're willing to give up in order to preserve that priority.

It took me a while to wrap my head around the projection concept (pun intended), but I think I understand it. Well, mostly.


THE PROBLEM TO SOLVE

What is the difference between world maps using different CRS?

Goode Homosoline: The sizes of each continent are accurate in proportion to the others, but distances are distorted especially near the poles

Equidistant Cylindrical: Navigation is easier (north and south stay constant) but latitudes are equally spaced, giving more accurate representation of distance along the north-south axis

Mercator: Better for navigation (north and south stay constant like equidistant cylindrical), but greater distortion at the poles makes continents farther from the Equator seem bigger than they actually are

Robinson: Less distortion in the middle of the map as compared to Mercator, and the poles have more severely curved lines for longitude, so the distances between continents can be grasped more accurately

THE STORY OF THE Hispanic population MAPS

Can a map answer a simple question, like how many Hispanic people are in the US, in more than one way? I've been interested in social psychology's "nudge theory" for a long time - basically, the environment we find ourselves in has a huge impact on our decisions.

The same concept applies to making maps. The exercises that led to the creation of these maps made me think about how a small tweak--of a graduated color ramp, symbology, or data classification break--can significantly change the perception for a map's viewer. When looking at the US Hispanic population size on a state-by-state basis, Illinois being in the top five while New Mexico was only 9th surprised me. When I dug deeper, I realized that with more than 12 million people in Illinois compared to New Mexico's 2 million, the number of Hispanic people would be greater in Illinois, while the rate of Hispanic people as a percentage of population would be much greater in New Mexico.

After looking at data classification strategies, I have a more sophisticated understanding of how population size can look different than density, which can also look different than rate (percent of total population). Depending on the story you want to tell, a map can look very different, and this makes map design a powerful communication tool.

Final thoughts

Much of what I gained from this class is not limited to GIS or even to geography, but centers on how visual communication methods can tell stories in a powerful way. Each time the same data was displayed in a different way -- proportional symbol as opposed to dot density, census-tract-level as opposed to state-level choropleth, even choice of graduated colors -- the map can imply emphasis, create an argument or even risk producing bias.

The other important skill I developed in this class is tenacity. For me the biggest difficulty was getting back onto my laptop the next day after I had stayed up late struggling to figure out a feature of ArcGIS. It was incredibly difficult to keep trying again and again, but it did feel great when I finally broke through to he other side.
